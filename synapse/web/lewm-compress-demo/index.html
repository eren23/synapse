What is LEWM?

LEWM (Learned World Model) is a vision-based world model that learns to predict future latent states from images and actions. It uses a ViT encoder (12 layers, 192 hidden) and a DiT predictor with adaLN modulation (6 layers, 1024 inner dim). The full f32 model is ~52MB of weights. Compression matters because this runs entirely in the browser via WASM — smaller downloads mean faster cold starts, and lower memory means it works on more devices.

Pruning Impact

What is Wanda pruning?

Wanda (Pruning by Weights and Activations) zeroes out the least important weights in each row of a weight matrix, ranked by |weight| x ||activation||. Unlike magnitude-only pruning, Wanda accounts for how much each weight actually contributes during inference, producing better quality at the same sparsity level.

Why are pruned models the same size on disk?

Q4 quantization packs weights into fixed-size 32-element blocks. Pruned (zeroed) weights still occupy their slots in these blocks, so the raw binary is the same size. However, blocks full of zeros compress dramatically under gzip or brotli — meaning HTTP transfer with Content-Encoding: br can be significantly smaller for pruned variants.

Quality vs. sparsity trade-off

  • 20% pruning — barely noticeable quality loss (cos 0.997 vs 0.998 for unpruned Q4). A safe default that compresses better over the wire.
  • 40% pruning — measurable but still usable quality degradation (cos 0.976). Good for bandwidth-constrained deployments where approximate rollouts are acceptable.
  • Pruning is applied before Q4 quantization, so the two compressions compound without conflicting.
